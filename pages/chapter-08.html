Entry 08 — Globalising

Chapter 7 — Malaysia Globalized, Globalising

Globalization usually gets talked about as if some countries are simply on the receiving end of change while others are the ones creating it. The reality is a lot more interesting than that. Countries don't just absorb global influences anymore, they adapt them, reshape them and eventually become global influences themselves.

Pieterse explains this by separating the idea of being globalized from globalizing. There is a huge difference between borrowing ideas from somewhere else and actively shaping globalization yourself. Countries like China are used throughout the chapter to show how that shift can happen over time. Rather than simply copying Western development, China selectively borrowed what worked, improved it and eventually began influencing other countries in return.

Borrowing Doesn't Mean Copying

One idea that stood out to me was the idea of "smart borrowing." Every country learns from someone else at some point. Technology, business models, education systems and even government institutions have all travelled across borders throughout history. The important part isn't who invented something first, but how another country chooses to adapt it for its own needs.

"Hybridization also means tweaking and repurposing." Pieterse, Globalization and Culture: Global Mélange, Chapter 8.

I think this quote sums up globalization perfectly. Most countries aren't interested in making exact copies of another culture or system. Instead, they take ideas that already exist and make them work for their own society. That's probably why globalization never looks exactly the same in every country.

Globalization Isn't One-Way Traffic

Something I found interesting was how the chapter slowly shifts the conversation away from Western dominance. While Europe and the United States have undeniably shaped globalization over the last few centuries, Pieterse argues that the balance has started changing. Emerging economies now have far more influence than they did before, especially through technology, manufacturing and global investment.

Instead of asking how countries are being globalized, it becomes more interesting to ask how they are globalizing everyone else. Looking at it this way makes globalization feel much less one-sided than I originally imagined.

Key Idea Globalized vs Globalizing is the difference between passively receiving global influences and actively shaping the direction of globalization through innovation, adaptation and economic influence.

Malaysia's Own Version of Smart Borrowing

Reading this made me think about Malaysia as well. We have always borrowed ideas from different cultures, whether it was through trade, colonial history, education or technology. The interesting part is that those influences rarely stay exactly the same after arriving here.

Malaysian food is probably one of the easiest examples. Chinese, Indian, Malay and countless other traditions have blended together over generations until they became something uniquely Malaysian. The same thing can be said for language, architecture and even the way businesses operate today.

Personal thoughts

I think this chapter pairs really well with the braid analogy from before. Every country borrows something, but every country also gives something back. Malaysia might not be one of the world's largest economies, but I think we still contribute our own culture, ideas and innovations to the global stage. That's what makes globalization feel less about competition and more about collaboration.

Notes

  1. Jan Nederveen Pieterse, Globalization and Culture: Global Mélange, Chapter 8, "Globalized, Globalizing." (pp. 103–107)

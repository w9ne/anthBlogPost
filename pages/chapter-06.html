Entry 06 — Capital

Chapter 5 — Deeper into Malaysian Hybridity

Before taking the class, I always thought cultural mixing was something that mostly happened because of modern globalization. However, Pieterse argues that hybridity has always existed. What globalization has changed is not the existence of cultural mixing, but rather the speed and scale at which it happens today.

Throughout the chapter, Pieterse challenges the idea that cultures were once completely pure and isolated from one another. Migration, conquest, religion, and travel have connected societies for thousands of years. Rather than hybridity as something new, he describes it as a normal part of human history that has essentially become more transparent in the future. (Chapter 6, pg 72)

Cultures Have Always Been Connected

One idea that stood out to me is that globalization did not suddenly create cultural exchange. According to Pieterse, people have been moving, trading, sharing ideas, and influencing one another long before the internet or even modern nation-states existed. You can see this effect with Malaysia's history, with influences of the Portugese, British and Japanese. Technologies, languages and religious customs have been passed around throughout history, making cultural mixing an ordinary part of human development rather than an exception for specfic coutries. In my option, it relates heavily to whether it was received well based on a people's perspective.

"Not mixing is new but the scope and speed of mixing." Pieterse, Globalization and Culture: Global Mélange, Chapter 6.

This quote is why I think this class exists today. The speed of which this mixing is happening is at an astounding rate. With the use of social media and the idea of being able to see a traditional dance created 100 years ago at the comfort of our beds is mind-boggling.

Hybridity Doesn't Mean Losing Identity

Hybridity doesn't necessarily mean culture dissapear. Some may feel like it has corrupted or dampened an experience of a culture. Instead, people continue creating new identities by combining different experiences, traditions, and influences. Pieterse even points to examples of countries and communities that openly recognize their mixed cultural identities rather than pretending they come from a single origin. Personally I think that some part of it is true, but at the same time I think culture will always evolve as time passes.

Key Idea Hybridity is not the replacement of one culture by another. It describes how people continuously combine different cultural influences while creating new identities of their own.

The deeper look into Malaysian Hybridity

It would be impossible to describe Malaysian culture as completely separate from the rest of the world purely because cultural exchange has always been an essential part of the country's history. Communities have interacted through all sorts of mediums for generations. This level of cultural mixing still exists today.

Rather than seeing hybridity as a negative conotation in which it pushes the narrative that Malaysia has lost its purest form of "culture", it becomes hard to imagine what Malaysia would be without the idea of cultural mixing.

Why I Think This Matters

This chapter made me rethink the way I understand globalization. Instead of asking whether globalization is destroying culture, I think a better question is how cultures continue adapting while remaining meaningful to the people who practice them. Pieterse argues that boundaries have always existed, but so has movement across those boundaries. Globalization simply makes those connections easier to see today. (Chapter 3)

Personal thoughts

I think that hybridity is a beautful thing and I think that it Malaysia is a shining example of how it has suceeeded in creating a safe space for all cultures, traditions and religions to bloom. Although there is definitely some pushback from those with a purist perspective, the overall majority loves the mixing, tangling and blending of culture as a whole.
